TASK 3: Multi-Level / Taxonomic Association Rules

1. WHAT THE APPROACH DOES

Multi-level (taxonomic) association rule mining, introduced by Srikant and Agrawal (1995) in "Mining Generalized Association Rules," extends the classical Apriori algorithm by incorporating item category hierarchies (taxonomies). In standard association rule mining, rules are discovered at a single level of item specificity (e.g., "customers who buy 2% milk also buy white bread"). Taxonomic association rules allow items to be grouped into hierarchies — for instance, {2% milk, whole milk} → Milk → Dairy — and discovers rules at multiple levels of abstraction. This produces rules like "customers who buy Dairy products also buy Bakery products," alongside more specific item-level rules.

2. HOW IT WORKS

The approach requires a predefined taxonomy: a directed acyclic graph (typically a tree) where leaf nodes are individual items and internal nodes represent categories at increasing levels of generality. The algorithm proceeds as follows:

First, each transaction is "extended" by adding all ancestors of each item in the taxonomy. If a transaction contains {2% milk, white bread}, it becomes {2% milk, Milk, Dairy, white bread, Bread, Bakery}. This extended transaction database is then mined using the Apriori algorithm with minimum support and confidence thresholds.

To handle the combinatorial explosion from added taxonomy nodes, the algorithm employs optimizations: (a) it prunes candidates that contain both an item and its ancestor, since such rules are redundant (e.g., "Milk → Dairy" is trivially true); (b) it uses the antimonotonicity property at each level — if a category does not meet minimum support, none of its sub-categories in combination with other items need to be checked.

The result is a set of rules at different levels of the hierarchy. A post-processing step filters out "uninteresting" rules where a more general version of the rule exists with similar or higher confidence.

3. PROS

- Discovers general patterns: Rules at higher taxonomy levels (e.g., "Electronics → Accessories") capture broader purchasing behaviors that item-level mining would miss due to low per-item support.
- Reduces redundant rules: Instead of hundreds of rules about individual products that essentially say the same thing, the taxonomy collapses them into concise, general rules.
- More actionable for decision-making: Category-level rules are often more useful for business strategy (e.g., store layout, marketing campaigns) than item-level rules.
- Increases effective support: Items that individually fall below the support threshold can be detected when aggregated into their parent categories.

4. CONS

- Requires a predefined taxonomy: The hierarchy must be specified by domain experts before mining. If the taxonomy is poorly designed or doesn't reflect true item relationships, the resulting rules may be misleading.
- May miss item-specific patterns: Aggregation to category level can obscure important associations between specific items. For example, if only one specific brand drives an association, the category-level rule hides this.
- Choice of hierarchy level matters: Rules mined at too high a level become trivially general ("Food → Food"), while too-specific levels revert to standard Apriori. Selecting the right granularity requires experimentation or domain knowledge.
- Computational overhead: Extending transactions with all ancestors increases the effective database size and the number of candidate itemsets, though the pruning optimizations partially mitigate this.
- Taxonomy maintenance: As product catalogs evolve, the taxonomy must be updated, adding an ongoing maintenance burden that standard Apriori does not have.
